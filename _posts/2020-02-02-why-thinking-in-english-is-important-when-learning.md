## Learning Concepts can be as easy as learning English words!

<p>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; You arrive at your dorm and toss your bag by the side of your bed. You pump yourself up... you have to as time is of the essence and you are supposed to submit an assignment on how to build chatbots in two days. There's just one problem -- you haven't even studied anything about the math required to build bots nor have you touched your expensive books to learn how AI are designed and developed!</p>

Time to make some coffee, get your books and begin your exploration of the topic at hand so that you can make something yourself. As you begin turning the pages of your 9000+ page book, you begin to feel despair envelop you. "Why do they have to use so much jargon!" you say, "Can the same concepts not be explained using simpler words?". I'm sure a lot of STEM students can relate -- maybe not to the story but at why textbooks have so many tough words and why learning something new feels so hard.

Well, I'd like to you hold that thought while I show you how learning new concepts can be as simple as learning English vocabulary and that if the next time when you come across a difficult word or phrase, instead of feeling lost, if you break it down and understand the words, you can learn a concept (or at the very least the gist of it) before you even begin.

Lets say you are learning about probability and you come across terms like Binomials, Normal Distribution, combinatorics, hyperbolic and the like. They may be easy enough to decipher on their own, but if they occur together in a long sentence, they often make our head spin. Those of you reading this may retort "These terms are easy... I already know what they mean", but you forget that the first time you saw them they made you queasy. If you knew that hyperbolic means excessive/extreme from the greek word _hyperbole_ meaning excess, then you might even be able to visualize what a graph (derived from another greek term _graphos_ meaning (something) drawn or written) of a hyperbola looks like! A graph that goes to the extremes probably goes to infinity. Similarly, Binomial breaks down to bi meaning two and nomial which is just another word for name. Once you substitute with simpler words and/or break down the word and infer context and meaning from their roots, prefixes and suffixes, the toughest of words will seem easy to understand.

> You said I needed to learn English not English, Greek, Latin and French!

Well, to my credit I did say it was as simple as learning English words and left the door open for English words derived from foreign languages.

> I mean this all seems so simple Anurag, tell me something substantial!

Are you sure that this insight isn't of significant value? Whenever we endeavour to learn something new, we tend to look at difficult terms and jargon as if they were impossible to comprehend and as if a PhD. in reading scientific texts is needed to grasp what the textbook is trying to convey. Our attitude towards difficult words and words unique to math or science might prevent us from learning thoroughly and may make you feel lost when you are reading a textbook to learn something new. All you need to do is to calm down and treat jargon like you would any other word and break it down and simplify. If you do this, you'll be able to infer from word roots, prefixes and suffixes the meaning of the word and, almost all of the time, the meaning of the term as used in Mathematics and Science. Mathematicians and Scientists unlike lawyers, like to name new ideas and objects as simply and obviously as possible good examples of which are "Black Holes" and "Neutron Stars" from the field of cosmology. In medicine, there a lot of difficult words but once you learn the word roots, prefixes and suffixes, it is easy to maintain a large vocabulary. "Hypoglycemia" for example is made up of three parts: _hypo_ meaning less, _gluco_ meaning sugar and _emia_ meaning presence in blood. I'm sure if you know that _natrium_ is the latin counterpart to the English word Sodium, you can infer the meaning of hyponatremia yourself!

## Where this falls short

You might think that a Normal Distribution (also called the Bell Curve), was so named because the probability distribution of a lot of phenomena in nature are distributed in a bell-curve like fashion where most of the frequency reduces rapidly as we move toward either extreme. While a lot of things do tend to follow this distribution, the name became widely adopted and circulated in the statistical community after Karl Pearson referred to the Gaussian Error Curve as a _Normal Curve_ <sup>[1](https://condor.depaul.edu/ntiourir/NormalOrigin.htm)</sup>.

## Words are powerful

Words are the symbols which we use to express ourselves. The importance of words in relation to our ability to learn and it's link with our ability to form thoughts and categorise the world around us was best showcased in the research done on primitive tribes by Jules Davidoff, Ian Davies and Debi Roberson. They showed that the Berinmo, a stone-age tribe in New Guinea, are unable to distinguish between blue and green while they can distinguish between 'nol' and 'wor' two colours that the rest of us see as variants of the same colour <sup>[2](https://www1.icsi.berkeley.edu/~kay/berinmo.pdf)</sup>. Our ability to categorise, form abstractions and think is highly interlinked with our vocabulary. For this reason, I always enjoy learning new words and I hope that you do too.
